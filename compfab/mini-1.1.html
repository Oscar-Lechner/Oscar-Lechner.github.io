Project Title

Assignment Overview

In this assignment, we explore real-world examples of computational design, both in nature and in human-made environments. We also highlight prominent researchers and designers whose work embodies the principles of generative and computational fabrication processes.

Task 1: Computational Design in Nature

We are asked to find and document examples of computational design found in the natural world. Specifically, we need one example each from:

  • Botanical World
  • Zoological World
  • Environments Built by (non-human) Organisms

Subtask 1.1: Botanical World — Fibonacci Spirals in Sunflowers

The arrangement of seeds in a sunflower head is a classic example of computational design in nature. The seeds follow a spiral pattern often referred to as the Fibonacci sequence (or golden spiral). This spiral maximizes packing efficiency, ensuring the plant utilizes available space for seed growth.



**Why It's Computational**: The pattern can be modeled using parametric equations or algorithmic rules that reflect the plant’s growth process.

References:
• Ball, P. (2009). Nature's Patterns: A Tapestry in Three Parts. Oxford University Press.
• “Fibonacci Phyllotaxis in Sunflowers.” (AskNature.org)

Subtask 1.2: Zoological World — Zebrafish Stripe Patterns

Zebrafish (Danio rerio) display distinctive horizontal stripes formed by pigment cells. These stripe patterns are understood to be generated by a reaction-diffusion mechanism—a concept first proposed by Alan Turing to describe how simple chemical interactions can create complex patterns.



**Why It's Computational**: The stripes arise from algorithmic interactions of pigment cells, where each cell’s behavior depends on local chemical gradients—a naturally occurring computational process.

References:
• Kondo, S. & Asal, R. (1995). A reaction-diffusion wave on the skin of the marine angelfish Pomacanthus. Nature, 376(6543), 765–768.
• Parichy, D. M. (2003). Pigment patterns: Fish in stripes and spots. Current Biology, 13(24).

Subtask 1.3: Environment Built by Organisms — Termite Mounds

Termite mounds are sophisticated structures incorporating vents and tunnels that maintain stable internal temperatures. The mound’s shape and ventilation system result from a complex feedback process among thousands of termites, each following local rules.



**Why It's Computational**: Building decisions are made collectively, driven by pheromone gradients and temperature/humidity cues. The result is an emergent structure that exemplifies computation through local interaction.

References:
• Turner, J.S. (2000). The Extended Organism: The Physiology of Animal-Built Structures. Harvard University Press.
• Inward, D., Beccaloni, G., & Eggleton, P. (2007). Death of an order: A comprehensive molecular phylogenetic study confirms that termites are eusocial cockroaches.

Task 2: Computational Design in the Human-made Built Environment

We now look at three examples of computational design in architecture, engineering, and other human-made artifacts. These examples illustrate how algorithmic, parametric, and generative approaches shape our built environment.

Subtask 2.1: Al Bahar Towers, Abu Dhabi

The Al Bahar Towers feature a dynamic facade controlled by a computer system that opens and closes in response to sunlight. This results in an adaptive shading system that reduces interior heat gain.



**Why It's Computational**: The facade’s geometry is generated through parametric modeling and is constantly adjusted via real-time sensors and algorithms.

References:
• Aedas. (n.d.). Al Bahar Towers project.
• Architectural Review: Al Bahar Towers

Subtask 2.2: The Sagrada Família, Barcelona

Antoni Gaudí’s Sagrada Família incorporates a hybrid of computational thinking—long before modern computers. Current architects use parametric software to complete Gaudí’s vision, employing advanced geometry to craft the cathedral’s intricate forms.



**Why It's Computational**: Gaudí initially used hanging chains and mirror models to derive catenary curves; now parametric modeling software (like Rhino/Grasshopper) helps replicate and refine these forms.

References:
• Burry, M. (2007). Scripting Cultures: Architectural Design and Programming. John Wiley & Sons.
• Official Sagrada Família Website

Subtask 2.3: 3D-Printed Bridge by Joris Laarman Lab

Joris Laarman Lab developed a fully 3D-printed steel bridge installed in Amsterdam. Robots welded steel layers in mid-air, creating complex geometric forms without the constraints of traditional casting or milling methods.



**Why It's Computational**: Every aspect of the bridge’s geometry was computationally designed and digitally fabricated with robotic arms, using advanced parametric and algorithmic modeling to optimize the structure.

References:
• Joris Laarman Lab. (n.d.). Official Website
• “MX3D Bridge.” (Architecture and Digital Fabrication Journals)

Task 3: Researchers/Artists/Designers of Computational Fabrication

Here, we highlight two practitioners whose work embodies the merging of creative processes, scientific research, and advanced fabrication technologies.

Subtask 3.1: Dr. Neri Oxman

Dr. Neri Oxman, formerly at MIT’s Media Lab, is renowned for her work in Material Ecology—combining design, biology, computing, and materials engineering. She has created 3D-printed structures inspired by nature’s geometries, using multi-material digital fabrication techniques.



**Process**: Oxman’s process involves algorithmic modeling to generate forms that mimic cellular structures or organic growth patterns. She integrates living organisms into some projects (e.g., synthetic apiaries) to explore new bio-composite materials.

**Why It's Interesting**: The cross-pollination of biology and computational methods paves the way for new sustainable manufacturing approaches.

References:
• Oxman, N. et al. (2010). Material Ecology. MIT Media Lab.
• Mediated Matter Group

Subtask 3.2: Joris Laarman

Joris Laarman is a Dutch designer who pushes the boundaries of computational fabrication. His studio has produced algorithmically-designed furniture and pioneered large-scale 3D printing with projects like the MX3D Bridge in Amsterdam.



**Process**: Laarman Lab uses custom software algorithms for generative design, then fabricates pieces using robotic arms that build up metal in free space layer by layer.

**Why It's Interesting**: The Lab’s approach merges aesthetics with advanced robotics, illustrating how computation can break free from traditional molds and geometry constraints.

References:
• Laarman, J. (n.d.). Joris Laarman Lab
• Designboom. (2018). “World’s first 3D-printed steel bridge installed in Amsterdam.”
